Name styles (multi-select) decide what gets generated: Real words (single dictionary words), Two words, Compound (curated brandable word pairs), Brandable (invented, pronounceable), Invented (modern vowel-ending coinages), Short & punchy (4–5 letters), and Prefix / Suffix patterns. Tick any combination; the pool is built from all of them, and names matching your keyword or its synonyms are ranked first.

The search scores every name, then checks domains best-first against public DNS until it fills How many to find in the states ticked under Find — or runs out. Short real-word .coms are mostly registered, so for lots of open names lean on Brandable / Invented / Short, add .io or .ai, or allow longer names.

The value score (0–100) is led by length and word quality — a short single real word scores far above a long coined string — then adjusted for extension, pronounceability, cleanliness (hyphens and numbers hurt), and a commercial-keyword boost (AI, crypto, health, lending). It rates brandability, not a guaranteed price. Est. resale is a separate rough dollar range estimated from those same traits, calibrated to real aftermarket patterns (3-letter .coms ~$10–50K, 4-letter ~$1–10K, one-word .coms much higher, gibberish under $100). It is an algorithm, not a live appraisal or a comp lookup — treat it as a ballpark and check NameBio for real comparable sales before pricing anything. States: open means unregistered — register it today at any registrar (confirmed with a second DNS lookup so it's trustworthy); for sale means parked at a marketplace, buyable at the owner's price; taken means registered and not listed; unverified means DNS was ambiguous (grouped with taken, off by default). Open and For sale are the ones you can buy. Confirm on Namecheap before paying.

More buying options. Open names link out to several registrars — Namecheap, Porkbun, Dynadot, GoDaddy — so you can cross-check availability and compare registration prices, since a name not showing on one sometimes shows on another. For-sale names get a tile listing the marketplaces they may be sold on (Sedo, Afternic, Dan, GoDaddy, Atom) in one place. Live asking prices can't be read in the browser (marketplaces block cross-site requests and there's no free price feed), so the tile links out to each listing; pulling real prices and merging duplicate listings needs those marketplaces' APIs through a backend.
